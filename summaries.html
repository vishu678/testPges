Improve Indoor Air Quality: Reducing Contaminants and Encouraging Good Ventilation in Public Facilities

2025-05-28 | Web Article

The Deschutes County Environmental Health Department has introduced an Indoor Air Quality (IAQ) Program aimed at improving the health of citizens by reducing exposure to indoor air contaminants. This program offers advice on potential indoor air hazards and resources for testing, prevention, and mitigation. The focus is on vulnerable populations in businesses, organizations, daycares, schools, healthcare settings, and other public facilities.

To combat the transmission of COVID-19, ensuring proper ventilation and adequate airflow is crucial. Simple actions like opening windows or using exhaust fans can help reduce the risk of viral transmission while improving overall air quality and lessening exposure to various indoor air hazards such as wildfire smoke.

In case of smoky outdoor conditions or poor air quality, Deschutes County offers technical assistance to improve indoor air quality through phone consultations or site visits. To request help, individuals must fill out an Indoor Air Quality Assistance Request Form. The assessments provided by the program offer a generalized air quality profile but do not provide professional HVAC or engineering evaluations.

In terms of environmental protection efforts related to temperature and humidity, the American Society of Heating, Refrigerating, and Air Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE) provides guidelines that recommend maintaining temperatures between 68 F to 74 F in winter and 72 F to 80 F in summer. Relative humidity should be maintained at a level between 30% and 60%. Lower humidity may discourage pests, mold, and bacterial growth, while higher humidity encourages virus particles to decay faster, potentially reducing the risk of infection.

The report also highlights two gases of concern: Carbon Monoxide (CO) and Carbon Dioxide (CO2). CO is an odorless, colorless gas produced by incomplete fuel burning that can cause symptoms such as fatigue, chest pain, headaches, dizziness, confusion, nausea, flu-like symptoms, and even be fatal at high concentrations. On the other hand, CO2 is a natural component of exhaled breath but also results from fossil fuel burning. High levels of CO2 can cause headache, dizziness, nausea, and even asphyxiation at extremely high concentrations.

Airborne particulate matter (PM2.5/PM10) caused by wildfire smoke and vehicle exhaust is another concern. These tiny particles can penetrate deep into the lungs and affect both the heart and lungs. Children, older adults, and people with heart or lung diseases are at greater risk from PM exposure.

Lastly, Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) found in many household products can off-gas into indoor air. These chemicals, which may not be easily detectable by smell, can pose health risks when inhaled over extended periods. Common examples of VOCs include benzene, formaldehyde, and toluene.

To address these issues, the report encourages taking proactive measures such as improving ventilation, maintaining appropriate temperature and humidity levels, ensuring proper HVAC systems, minimizing CO emissions, managing CO2 concentrations, reducing particulate matter exposure, and avoiding excessive use of products containing VOCs. The IAQ program offers assistance in identifying sources, potential remedies, and providing referrals to professional HVAC resources.
